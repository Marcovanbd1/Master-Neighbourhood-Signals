FAQs

The point of this page is to remove friction for commissioners and partners. If you can’t find the question you need, it should be added.

Neighbourhood Signals only works if it is easy to understand, safe to trust, and simple to start.
What is Neighbourhood Signals, in one sentence?

A public, safe civic listening layer that turns aggregated navigation behaviour into a readable Neighbourhood Brief, so partners can act earlier, together, without personal data.

Is this surveillance?

No. It does not identify people, infer identity, or track individuals. If something requires personal data to be useful, it does not belong in Neighbourhood Signals.

Why is the Brief public?

Publicness is a safety mechanism. It prevents hidden dashboards, private interpretation, and quiet mission creep, and it makes shared stewardship possible.

What are Neighbourhood Colours?

A blinding convention for discussing signal clusters while geography stays masked. This prevents stigmatising or politicised “place judgement” until an agreed action genuinely requires location.

Does the 30-day sprint lock us into anything?

No. The sprint is the smallest possible commitment: listen for 30 days, publish the first Brief, then decide what depth of listening you want next.

Is 30 days long enough?

Yes for a first signal picture and a first Brief. Some signals will be clearer than others, and the Brief is explicit about uncertainty. The point is to create evidence for commissioning, not perfection.

Who publishes the Brief?

The Neighbourhood Steward publishes it on behalf of the local partnership, alongside the public covenant. Neighbourhood CoLab CIC can support drafting and publication patterns.

What kinds of actions come out of the Brief?

Low-regret actions first: navigation fixes, clearer signposting, small access improvements, shared comms, better pathways between services, and sometimes placing physical listening points where people actually are.

What is “unblinding” and when does it happen?

Unblinding is the governed moment when a location is revealed because a specific agreed action genuinely requires knowing where. It is minimal, logged, and auditable.

How is this commissioned after the sprint?

By choosing listening depth and response cadence. The sprint produces your local evidence, then you commission the tier that matches how closely you want to listen and how actively you want to respond.

What is Neighbourhood CoLab CIC’s role?

The quiet delivery partner. It carries the safety model, stewardship patterns, civic infrastructure components, and learning between places, without needing to be the public face of the work.
